Hi, my name is Wolf De Wulf and I am a computer scientist from Brussels.  
I studied artificial intelligence (AI) at the 
[Vrije Universiteit Brussel](https://www.vub.be), and I am currently researching AI in biomedical contexts, as part of the 2022 cohort of the [UKRI CDT in Biomedical AI](https://web.inf.ed.ac.uk/cdt/biomedical-ai) at the [University of Edinburgh](https://www.ed.ac.uk/).

I am pursuing a PhD in computational neuroscience, jointly supervised by [Professor Matthias Hennig](https://homepages.inf.ed.ac.uk/mhennig/) and [Professor Matthew Nolan](https://nolanlab.mvm.ed.ac.uk/). The project I work on investigates reward information in the MEC and how it might drive goal-directed navigation. By working closely with the experimentalists in the Nolan lab, we get to analyse neural activity recordings from mice performing a spatial navigation task. We typically start by looking for neurons tuned to landmarks or events, applying rigorous statistical testing to eliminate confounds. By comparing groups of neurons between different experimental conditions (e.g. trials in which a reward is delivered or not), we eliminate hypotheses and update our models. We can then verify findings by performing dimensionality reduction techniques on the neuronal data and investigating how the topology of the manifold might reflect the hypothesised computations. In parallel, we can compare the results of each of these analyses with results obtained from artificial neuronal activity, generated by recurrent neural networks that we try model to be anatomically similar to those in the MEC and/or hippocampus.

Investigating how circuits of neurons interact to form intelligence is a fundamental aspect of both neuroscience and artificial intelligence (AI) research. On the one hand, understanding the neural codes behind a particular behaviour facilitates the development of treatments for conditions related to that behaviour. On the other, it can yield inspiration towards novel AI models that mimic the brain. Ultimately, our research might facilitate investigations into how navigation deficits arise in neural disorders such as early-stage Alzheimer’s disease and autism spectrum disorder.
